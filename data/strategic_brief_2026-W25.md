## 5. Three Things Listn Should Do Differently

### #1 — Lead with The Moment, Not The Product

Every competitor advertises the **output** (the book, the device) or the **problem** (80% of stories never get made, parents feel alone). Nobody is advertising the **moment of discovery** — what it feels like when a parent tells you a story about their life that you never knew existed.

**What Listn should do:** Build a creative track centered on the adult child's experience of listening — not the end product. The hero of the ad is the 45-year-old hearing their father describe what it felt like to emigrate at 22, or their mother's first heartbreak. That moment of surprise and connection is Listn's unique competitive ground because Listn is voice-first — the conversation *is* the product, not just the input.

**Example creative direction (addressed to adult child buyer):**
> *"You've talked to your dad your whole life. But you've never asked him these questions. Listn gives you 30 guided conversations — and the recordings to keep forever."*

The parent appears in the ad (their voice, their face), but the buyer is the adult child experiencing something new.

---

### #2 — Make "Voice" a Concrete Emotional Differentiator, Not a Feature Label

Heritage Whisper says "voice-first" as if it's a spec sheet. Remento calls it "Speech-to-Story" — still functional. StoryKeeper mentions voice as one capability among several. Nobody has made **the actual sound of a parent's voice** the emotional center of their advertising.

Listn is an audio-first product. The product delivers something no book can: the exact cadence, the laugh, the pause, the accent. That's irreplaceable in a way a transcribed book simply is not. Competitors are leaving this entirely unclaimed.

**What Listn should do:** Make "their voice" the lead emotional concept — not "their stories." A story can be retold. A voice disappears. Use this with precision and without melodrama. The ad copy shouldn't say "before it's too late" (fear-based, manipulative) — it should say something like:

> *"A book can carry their words. Only Listn keeps their voice."*

This directly attacks Remento and StoryKeeper's physical-book positioning by naming what a book cannot do.

---

### #3 — Ditch "Learn More" and Build a CTA That Invites Action Into the Process

"Learn More" is a category default that signals zero confidence in conversion. "Shop Now" (StoryKeeper) is transactional but cold for a product built on emotional intimacy. Neither fits Listn.

Listn's onboarding is itself emotionally meaningful — you're not buying a widget, you're starting a series of conversations. The CTA should make that feel real.

**What Listn should do:** Test CTAs like:
- **"Start the First Conversation"**
- **"Hear What You've Been Missing"**
- **"Give Dad His First Prompt"**

These CTAs are low-friction (they don't say "buy" or "pay") while being highly specific to the product experience. They also address the adult child buyer directly and frame the action as something they're *giving* — which matches the gift-purchase psychology that works for Remento and StoryKeeper.

## 6. Gaps Listn Can Own

### Gap #1 — Non-Occasion, Evergreen Urgency

Every competitor's emotional urgency is **calendar-dependent**: Father's Day, Mother's Day, holidays. Outside those windows, urgency disappears. Enna is the only brand running truly evergreen creative — but their urgency is zero (they're purely benefit-forward).

**The real urgency in this category is not calendar-based. It's biological.** A parent is getting older *right now*, not just on Father's Day. The adult child who sees a Listn ad in October needs a reason to act in October.

**The gap:** Evergreen creative that creates urgency around *time passing with a living parent* — not around a gift occasion. This is territory none of the competitors have claimed because it requires emotional precision to execute without manipulation.

**Listn's angle (addressed to adult child buyer):**
> *"Your dad is 74. He has stories you've never heard. Listn lets you start asking — tonight."*

That's urgency that doesn't require a holiday.

---

### Gap #2 — The "What You Don't Know" Hook

All competitor copy assumes the adult child buyer *knows* they want to capture a parent's stories. They address people who already feel the impulse. Nobody is creating the impulse in people who haven't felt it yet — a much larger pool of potential buyers.

**The gap:** Creative that reveals to the adult child what they *don't know* about their parent. This could be executed as:

- Questions the buyer has never thought to ask (e.g., "Do you know what your dad was doing the year before you were born?")
- Stories that exist but haven't been told (e.g., "There are 40 years of your mom's life you know almost nothing about")

This approach expands the market rather than competing for existing demand. It also directly positions Listn's guided-conversation feature as the reason you'd actually get those answers — because most adult children wouldn't know where to start asking on their own.

---

### Gap #3 — Grandchildren as the Emotional Stake-Holders

Remento briefly surfaces this with:

> *"My kids see their grandparents as heroes now!"*

...but it's buried as a headline, not developed as a creative theme. No competitor has built a campaign around **grandchildren who will never meet the grandparent they can still hear**.

**The gap:** Ads addressed to adult child buyers that foreground the grandchild's future relationship with an ancestor's voice. A 35-year-old parent sees themselves in this immediately — they are both the adult child of an aging parent AND the parent of a child who will someday wish they could hear great-grandparent's voice.

**Listn's angle:**
> *"Someday your kids will ask you what grandpa was really like. Listn means you'll have more than photos to show them."*

This is addressed squarely to the adult child buyer (35-55), features the older adult as the subject, and introduces the grandchild relationship as an emotional multiplier — without ever asking the grandparent to buy anything.

---

### Gap #4 — The "Easy for Them" Proof Point (Positioned as Love, Not Tech)

Enna's 227-day evergreen winner is built entirely on "no setup required." This works because adult children have experienced the pain of trying to teach a parent to use technology. Nobody in the memory preservation space has claimed this angle.

Remento's closest attempt:

> *"No downloads, logins, or passwords"*

...but this is presented as a feature bullet, not a lead emotional message. It's buried in body copy.

**The gap:** An ad that explicitly acknowledges the real fear — *I don't want to spend three hours on the phone helping Dad figure out the app* — and resolves it as an act of love rather than a technical specification. This is Listn's voice-first model's best-fit advantage: voice conversations require no tech literacy from the storyteller.

**Listn's angle:**
> *"Your mom doesn't need to download anything. She just needs to talk. Listn does the rest."*

This directly neutralizes the primary objection for adult child buyers who have tried and failed to get elderly parents engaged with technology — and positions voice-first as the emotional solution, not just the UX choice.
