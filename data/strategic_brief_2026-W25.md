## 5. Three Things Listn Should Do Differently

### 1. Own the "Voice" Differentiator Explicitly — and Make It the Hero

Competitors mention voice incidentally. Remento buries it:

> "Each story is paired with a QR code that when scanned, links back to original recordings."

StoryKeeper mentions it as a feature:

> "The Only Book That Let's You Listen to Their Story."

Nobody is making **the actual sound of a parent's voice** the emotional centerpiece of their advertising. This is Listn's core product truth and its most defensible differentiator. A parent's voice — their specific cadence, their laugh mid-story, the way they say certain words — is irreplaceable in a way that a transcribed book is not.

**Recommendation:** Build a creative around a single, concrete moment: *"You'll never hear her voice in a book."* Feature the emotional reality that a hardcover has no audio — and Listn does. Target the adult child (35–55) who has already lost a grandparent or knows someone who has. The older adult can appear in the creative (a face, a voice playing on a phone speaker), but the ad speaks directly to the buyer: *"Her voice, saved. Not just her words."*

---

### 2. Lead with Regret, Not Celebration

Every competitor in this dataset leads with the positive outcome: the book, the gift, the connection, the celebration. Nobody leads with the thing that *actually* creates urgency in the adult child buyer — the regret they'll feel if they don't act.

Remento gets closest with their stat:

> "80% of life story books never get made."

But this is a *product failure* framing, not a *personal regret* framing. It positions the guilt as belonging to a category statistic, not to the buyer sitting in front of the ad.

**Recommendation:** Run a creative that names the specific regret. Something like:

> *"I never asked him about the war."*
> *"She always said she'd tell me the whole story someday."*
> *"I didn't know that was the last time we'd talk about it."*

These are sentences every adult child has thought. No competitor is saying them out loud. The ad then turns: *"Listn gives you the conversation you keep meaning to have."* The older adult's face/voice can appear in the visual — but the copy is addressed entirely to the child who waited too long or hasn't yet.

---

### 3. Ditch "Learn More" — Use a CTA That Closes the Emotional Loop

Every competitor in this dataset is using "Learn More" or "Shop Now" as their call to action. These CTAs are designed for someone who's evaluating a product. But Listn's buyer isn't in product-evaluation mode — they're in *emotional recognition* mode. When the ad hits them, they're not thinking "I should research this." They're thinking "Oh god, I haven't done this yet."

**Recommendation:** Replace "Learn More" with a CTA that matches the emotional moment the ad creates. Options:

- **"Start the Conversation"** — action-oriented, directly describes the product experience, emotionally resonant
- **"Give Them a Voice"** — frames the adult child as the giver of something irreplaceable
- **"Capture Their Story"** — specific and active

The CTA should feel like the *first step* of the experience, not the first step of a purchase funnel. This matters especially on mobile where the CTA button is the last thing seen before a thumb moves on.

## 6. Gaps Listn Can Own

### Gap 1: The Non-Occasion Frame

Every single competitor in this dataset is running seasonal/occasion-based creative: Father's Day, Mother's Day, "the gift they'll never forget." This means the entire category is training its audience to think about memory preservation **only when a holiday forces them to**.

Listn can own the **non-occasion urgency frame** — the idea that the right time to start is *now*, not at Christmas or Father's Day. The adult child who sees a Remento ad in December and thinks "great gift idea" is not the same person as the adult child who sees a Listn ad on a random Tuesday in March and thinks "I need to do this before it's too late."

**Angle:** *"There's no occasion for this conversation — just the ones you'll wish you'd had."* This positions Listn as the brand that takes the underlying emotional stakes seriously, not just the gift-giving opportunity.

---

### Gap 2: The Grandchild Buyer as Gift-Giver

Remento's headline:

> "My kids see their grandparents as heroes now!"

...is the only ad in the entire dataset that hints at grandchildren. But it's used as *social proof from a parent*, not as a targeting angle in its own right.

The **adult grandchild (25–40) buying for a grandparent** is an underserved persona. They're often the most digitally fluent generation in the family. They feel the loss of grandparent stories acutely — they're old enough to appreciate what they don't know, young enough to still have access to a living grandparent. And critically: they're not thinking about this as a "gift for Dad" — they're thinking about this as *something they personally want*.

**Angle:** Address this buyer directly. *"Before you lose the chance to ask her everything you always wanted to know."* The copy speaks to the grandchild's curiosity and longing, not a gift occasion. This is both an emotional and strategic differentiator — no competitor is speaking to this buyer in this register.

---

### Gap 3: The "Already Regret" Buyer — Post-Loss Motivation for Future Relatives

Nobody in this dataset is speaking to the adult child who has *already lost* a parent and is now acutely aware of the stories that died with them — and who now has a surviving parent, in-law, or grandparent they haven't captured yet.

This person is not in a gift-buying mindset. They're in a quiet-grief-meets-resolve mindset. They are, arguably, the most motivated buyer in the entire space. The loss they experienced has already done the emotional convincing — they just need someone to hand them the solution.

**Angle:** A creative that opens with: *"When my dad died, I realized I'd never asked him ___."* This is intensely personal, requires no statistical framing, and speaks directly to the gut-level motivation that no competitor is addressing. The older adult featured in the ad is a *different* parent — the one still living — and the adult child is the buyer who won't make the same mistake twice.

> ⚠️ **Tone note:** This angle requires care. It should feel like empathy, not exploitation. The resolution should be hopeful — "You still have time with her" — not guilt-driven. Done right, it's the most powerful creative gap in the entire competitive landscape.

---

### Gap 4: The Ongoing Experience (vs. One-Time Gift)

Every competitor positions their product as a **one-time gift event**: buy it, record it, print it, done. The emotional framing is always a completed artifact — a book, a keepsake, a hardcover.

Listn's voice-first, conversation-based model is inherently *ongoing*. Multiple sessions. Multiple stories. A relationship that deepens over time.

No competitor is advertising the **continuous relationship** that their product could enable. This is a gap for Listn to claim: the idea that every conversation captures something new, and that the practice of recording stories itself changes how families relate to each other.

**Angle:** *"She tells a different story every time you ask."* Or: *"You've been doing this for three months, and she's only just getting to the good part."* This positions Listn not as a gift but as a **living family practice** — something you build over time rather than buy once. It's a harder sell on first contact but a more defensible brand position and a natural subscription/retention story.
